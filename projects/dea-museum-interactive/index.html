Telling the Story of the DEA

Redesigning the DEA Museum's website with a renewed focus on human-centered design, I enhanced user experience during the pandemic using visitor feedback and analytics to cater to diverse informational needs.

View⤴

The DEA Museum serves as a repository for artifacts and information related to drug policy, law enforcement, and the history of drug misuse and addiction in the U.S. It aims to educate the public through various mediums, including its physical and digital presence.
The project, while not the sexiest thing I’ve ever worked on, exemplified the importance of blending quantitative and qualitative research to understand the diverse needs of audiences, especially in challenging circumstances like the COVID-19 epidemic.

Unique visitor dynamics

Unlike typical museums where visitors actively decide to attend, the DEA Museum often sees group visits organized by educators or leaders. This unique dynamic influenced the strategic focus of the website, shifting from providing logistical information to supporting the educational goals of the groups and the DEA’s outreach programs.

Adapting to COVID-19 Restrictions

The team adapted to the pandemic’s limitations by employing a range of research and design methods, including market research, virtual site visits, website analytics, and surveys, to gather insights and inform the design process.

Content consumption

Analysis revealed that website visitors were more interested in the museum’s educational content, such as history and lectures, rather than logistical information like directions and hours.

From card sorting to information architecture

To better organize the website’s content, a card sorting exercise was conducted. This helped understand how different audiences categorize and prioritize museum-related information.
Identifying Primary and Secondary Groups
Through a similarity matrix and heuristic analysis, the team identified primary and secondary topic groups, influencing how the website’s content would be structured and interlinked.
Refining Information Architecture
The findings from the card sorting and clustering analysis were used to refine the website’s information architecture, ensuring that it resonates with the diverse needs and mental models of the museum’s visitors.

Being a reliable narrator

Voice of the Customer and Voice of Analytics

Visitor feedback (VoC) and digital analytics (VoA) helped us to identify different visitor categories and understand their digital interactions with the museum.

HCD research in the time of COVID

COVID and restrictions on social distancing limited the team’s ability to interact directly with audiences. Remote card sorting and treejack testing proved invaluable for developing a site structure that shortened the distance people traveled to find answers to their questions.
